Cultural effects on German PISA 2012 scores¶

by Markus Stachl¶

31.07.2021

Investigation Overview¶

PISA is the OECD's Programme for International Student Assessment. PISA measures 15-year-olds ability to use their reading, mathematics and science knowledge and skills to meet real-life challenges. I restricted the dataset to reponses from German students.

The main goal is to analyse how the cultural and educational backgroud of students influence their school performance indicated by the PISA test results. I also want to highlight possible struggles of immigrants in Germany.

Dataset Overview¶

The dataset contains full responses from 5000 German students to the PISA 2012 test survey. I restricted the feature-rich dataset to 11 features for this analysis.

How did German students do on PISA 2012?¶

German students on average obtained higher results compared to the OECD average. This is the case for all three test subjects.

Education stays in the family¶

A strong indicator of student PISA scores is their parents education. If at least one parent has some level of higher education, their kids tend to perform better on PISA tests. This might be due to the fact that parents with higher education typically want their kids to follow in their footsteps and give their kids the needed support.

Students, whos parents have not finished school, typically struggle in school as well.

First generation immigrants struggle, but there is hope¶

Around 10% of German students attending the survey are at least Second-generation immigrants

Immigrants on average scored about 10% less in every PISA 2012 test subject.

Second-generation immigrants can tighten the gap towards native students, suggesting that they can eliminate the initial handicap with the time being in Germany.

Surprisingly, reading does not seem to be a bigger obstacle than mathematics or science.

Family structures at home surprisingly hardly have effect on students' performances¶

Students living with a single parent perform equally well in all tests as their classmates living with both parents. It seems that most of single parents can handle the additional pressure pretty well.

Students living in non-tradional family structures (without parents, with grand-parents or similar) tend to struggle greatly in school. This might be due to a lack of an attachment figure.

This effect holds for both native and immigrated students.
